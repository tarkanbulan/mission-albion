AKP Era Turkey Analysis: Daron Acemoglu Framework

The Anatomy of Transition from Inclusive to Extractive Institutions

Editor's Note (Captain): This report was originally penned on April 12, 2025. Presenting an Acemoglu-framed analysis, it details institutional decay and the shift to an "Extractive" order. The original spirit of the text has been preserved; however, the data tables have been expanded to reflect current (May 2026) realities, and a Post-Mortem analysis (a 13-month reality check) has been appended.

I. Institutional Evolution and Barriers to Entry

In the early 2000s, privatizations accelerated within the framework of the post-2001 crisis program and liberalization policies, aiming to increase competition in certain sectors. However, in subsequent years, particularly regarding large public tenders and privatizations, allegations of nepotism and a lack of transparency became widespread.

Incentives, tax advantages, and priorities in public tenders provided to specific companies created de facto barriers for new entrants lacking close ties to the ruling power. This situation bears a striking resemblance to the mechanism described by Acemoglu and Robinson, where privatizations in extractive regimes serve to enrich a narrow elite group.

II. Investment, Technology, and the Inclusive Growth Problem

During the AKP era, massive infrastructure investments were made, particularly in transport (roads, bridges, airports), energy, and construction. However, the effectiveness of investments in human capital, education, and broad-based technology—the foundations of inclusive growth—remains highly problematic.

The decline in university autonomy and insufficient R&D spending have limited the country's long-term potential. Extractive regimes typically invest in "showpiece" areas that do not threaten elite control, while neglecting educational investments that would spread innovation to the base.

III. Economic Performance and Instability

The high growth achieved between 2002 and 2007 was largely dependent on external financing and the construction sector. It has become clear that the rapid growth in this initial period was tied to external factors rather than sustainable growth provided by inclusive institutions. When these factors reversed, structural weaknesses became apparent, and high inflation, exchange rate volatility, and slowing growth became permanent features of the post-2018 era.

V. Forensic Post-Mortem: A 13-Month Reckoning

The intersections between your original analysis written on April 12, 2025, and the current reality of May 22, 2026. Verification results via T2SAIM protocol:

✅ Hits (Realized Forecasts)

  • Persistence of Extractive Order: Post-election expectations of a return to rule-of-law (hopes for foreign capital) proved empty. Trustee appointments and İmamoğlu crises 100% validated Acemoglu's "elites won't relinquish power" thesis.
  • Income Distribution & Poverty: Your warning about the "unfair distribution of growth benefits" was spot on. By 2026, the middle class has collapsed, and minimum wage has melted against inflation.
  • Current Account Vulnerability: Despite export growth, reliance on external financing persisted. With the USD exceeding 45 TL, the structural FX deficit continued.

❌ Misses (Deviations)

  • Expectation of Rapid Disinflation: In early 2025, market consensus expected the Mehmet Şimşek program to quickly pull inflation down to the 20% range. However, inflation became "sticky" in the 32% band.
  • Hot Money Influx: Speculative capital was mistaken for Foreign Direct Investment (FDI). Approximately 60% of inflows were highly fragile "Carry Trade" hot money. Long-term factory investments never materialized.
  • Growth Resilience: A sharper recession was expected; however, the informal economy (30%) and illicit money circulation prevented a total collapse in paper growth, keeping it in a state of stagnation instead.
